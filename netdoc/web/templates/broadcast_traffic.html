What is broadcast traffic and why does it matter?
What is broadcast in practice?

Normal network traffic (unicast) goes from one device to one recipient. A broadcast packet is sent to everyone — every device on the same network must receive and process it, even if it doesn't need it.

Common broadcast sources: ARP protocol ("who has IP address X?"), DHCP (IP address requests), NetBIOS/LLMNR (legacy Windows name resolution), Spanning Tree (switches), OSPF/RIP routers, SCADA/BMS systems, printers and IP cameras (often excessive announcements).

When does broadcast become a problem?

Every broadcast reaches every device on the network — computers, cameras, phones, PLCs. With a dozen devices this is fine. With hundreds or thousands:

  • Device CPUs waste cycles processing junk traffic
  • Older switches and routers buffer and drop packets (packet drops)
  • IoT devices, PLCs and IP cameras may freeze or reboot
  • Throughput for real data traffic drops
  • On Wi-Fi, broadcast burdens all clients simultaneously
Solution: VLAN segmentation

A VLAN (Virtual LAN) creates separate broadcast domains — a broadcast sent in VLAN 10 will never reach VLAN 20. Devices on other VLANs don't even have to receive it.

Typical segmentation for a business network:

VLAN 10 — Office workstations VLAN 20 — IP cameras / CCTV VLAN 30 — Guest Wi-Fi VLAN 40 — Printers VLAN 50 — Automation / BMS / SCADA VLAN 99 — Network management
How to read this page?
  • Total In — total broadcast+multicast traffic received by the device in the selected time window. High value = this device is on a noisy network or is itself generating noise.
  • In Bcast / In Mcast — precise split (newer devices with SNMP v2+). High Bcast may indicate excessive ARP or a broadcast storm.
  • NUcast In — older combined counter (bcast+mcast together), for devices without full IF-MIB support.
  • Passive — packets captured passively by the NetDoc host — visible even without SNMP.
  • SPAM — device exceeded the configured threshold — candidate for investigation or isolation in a dedicated VLAN.

Tip: if one device generates significantly more broadcast than others — check for misconfiguration, a network loop (STP), or a faulty network card.

Rule of thumb: if the sum of Total In across all devices exceeds a few million packets per day and the network has no VLANs — segmentation is worth planning. IP cameras, BMS systems and IoT devices in separate VLANs can reduce network noise by 60–90%.
